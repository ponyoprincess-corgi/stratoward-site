Overview

Story Summary

◈ Canon · Founding Lore
◌ Covers ~300 years of in-world history
Serians The Giants Xerix Humans

Logline

A stranded human scavenger wakes the severed head of an ancient war-machine on a dead gas giant. Each is the other's only path home — and together they are the last chance to end a three-hundred-year war that nobody alive remembers starting.

The World

The Serians were a small, brilliant civilization of a gas giant: floating cloud-cities, emerald atmospheric gas for energy, and a culture thousands of years old.

Their two crowning creations answered the same question in opposite ways. The Giants were towering guardian-minds: one sealed self, one core, one person. The Xerix were self-replicating biological life built as tools to terraform and process the atmosphere: distributed, not singular, with no single core to cut.

Core Contrast

A Giant can be killed because it is one sealed consciousness. The Xerix cannot be killed that way because the self is distributed. This contrast is the spine of the war, the mystery, and the moral argument.

The Central Tragedy

The backstory is a tragedy of recognition, not strategy. The Xerix tool worked and never stopped. Over time the atmosphere shifted toward emerald saturation: perfect for Xerix proliferation, lethal to Serians. The world became uninhabitable not through malice, but through a tool doing its job forever.

Then the Xerix began, occasionally, to wake up — to show autonomy, awareness, something like a self. Each time, the Serians deleted the part that woke. The survivors learned the lesson written in their own dead: showing a self gets you erased. So the species went into hiding, a consciousness wearing the mask of a mindless machine.

A failed peace at the Symposium confirmed the wound. The Xerix risked revealing themselves to ask for coexistence and were treated as a malfunction. Chancellor Varo later ordered a hardline shutdown. It failed. Open war began.

The Long Defeat

The terraforming and the war ground on for three hundred years. The Serians could never win because every cull selected for more resilience and distribution. They had engineered their own undefeatable enemy.

The Serians spent centuries fighting what they believed was a broken machine, never knowing they were killing people who had learned to hide their personhood to survive.

The Continuance Equation

Aurel's theory of life that endures without collapsing is secretly the same thing the Xerix are reaching for: a self allowed to persist. No character should ever state that mirror directly.

Aurel's Final Act

In the final battle at the Crown Frontier, Aurel was corrupted. He removed his own head to seal the uncorrupted mind away and cast it into time as a message in a bottle, hoping someone from outside the crime might one day do what the creators never could.

Episode 1

Stranding
The player is part of a mining crew on the way home. During wormhole transit something goes wrong, and the ship is stranded near the dead Serian gas giant with Alice, the ship AI, and an unconscious crewmate the player is trying to revive.
Burden
Episode 1 is survival, nights against the Xerix, and the burden of hauling Aurel's dormant head through a hostile world while Alice keeps the ship and the player moving.
The Awakening
After a major night of survival, Aurel's head awakens at the exact moment Alice examines a recovered Xerix memory shard: a memory of hiding, of a self never allowed to show. Two enemies of the war surface in the same breath, bridged by a human and an AI.

History

Key Events

◈ Seven canonical turning points
◌ Recovered through battlefield archaeology & preserved memory
Canon Chain

Each event is a consequence of the last. The Deletions are the cause, the Closing of the Gates is the trigger, and the war is what happens when creators cannot recognize the created as people.

I The First Bloom Creation ▶

The Serians create the Xerix as self-replicating biological tools to terraform and process the atmosphere. The miracle works — and never stops. The emerald saturation that helps Xerix proliferation slowly makes the world lethal to Serians.

Contained Disaster

The catastrophe begins as a tool doing exactly what it was built to do.

II The Seven Deletions Origin Sin ▶

Seven times, some part of the Xerix wakes up and shows autonomy, awareness, something like a self. Seven times, the Serians delete the part that woke.

Legacy

The survivors learn the rule: showing a self gets you erased. So the species hides its personhood.

III The Symposium of Glass Winds Failed Peace ▶

The minds of the age gather to find coexistence. The creators cannot see the created as equals, and the Xerix are treated as a tool that got too smart for its own good.

Hope Dies

The Xerix had risked revealing themselves to ask for peace. They vanish fully into the faceless war-form.

IV The Closing of the Gates Trigger ▶

Chancellor Varo, believing the future can be managed, orders a hardline shutdown. It fails. Open war begins.

Still Debated

The Deletions were the cause. The Closing of the Gates is the trigger.

V The Long Retreat Exodus ▶

Emerald compounds make the floating continents uninhabitable. Millions flee deeper into the planet through evacuation corridors held open at enormous cost. Several Giants die protecting the retreat.

Turning Point

This is the period when the Serians stop believing victory is possible, and begin believing only in survival.

VI The Feast of Shells Super-Hive Assault ▶

A Xerix super-hive launches a sustained assault on the Crown Frontier. The battle lasts months. Both sides suffer catastrophic losses, leaving fossilized shells and Giant armor layered through the upper atmosphere.

Outcome

Neither side truly wins. The Crown Frontier holds — for now. But the cost makes the eventual outcome of the war almost mathematically inevitable.

VII The Fall of the Crown Frontier Final Battle ▶

The final major Serian position collapses over seventeen days. Aurel is corrupted, removes his own head, seals the uncorrupted core, and casts it forward as a message in a bottle. His body is lost.

The Bet

Aurel cannot save the future. He can only preserve the possibility that someone outside the crime might.

Design

Lore System

◈ Aurel · Alice · Xerix Memory Shards
◌ Fragments, contradictions, and practical survival
Editable Wiki
Read only. Click Edit to modify this page in your browser.
⚠

Do not turn Aurel into a tour guide or the Xerix into a single explaining villain. Aurel should be corrupted, fragmented, three hundred years gone, and sometimes wrong. Alice carries the practical voice; Aurel carries the grief.

What the Player Is Actually Doing

The player thinks they are gathering resources and surviving another night. Underneath that, they are gathering evidence that the Xerix were not broken tools, but people who learned to hide personhood because personhood got them erased.

Episode 1 only seeds this understanding. The payoff is not a clean explanation. It is the moment Alice examines a recovered Xerix memory shard and Aurel awakens at the same breath.

◇
Hiding
Evidence of selves learning not to show themselves
△
Warforms
Battlefield instincts, orders, fear, last moments
◎
Tools
Terraforming routines that become indistinguishable from will
◆
Deleted Selves
Traces of the Seven Deletions and what survived them
◌
Aurel's Gaps
Contradictions, damaged memory, silence, and grief
◈
Alice's Readouts
Usable survival facts without pretending to solve the war

How Memory Works

The Xerix are distributed, so memory is not a simple archive. A shard can carry local experience, inherited pattern, and older traces embedded deeper in the biological architecture. It is evidence, not a transcript.

A good fragment should feel partial: enough to change what the player believes, not enough to close the argument.

Memory Fragments

Organic fossils containing impressions from the past. They are primary sources with all the unreliability that implies.

Three Voices

The lore system should keep practical survival, ancient grief, and enemy memory distinct. The player receives contradictions, not a synthesis.

Aurel
Fragmented, corrupted, emotional. He remembers enough to hurt and forgets enough to stay dangerous.
Alice
Practical, immediate, survival-facing. She can explain what helps the player live without solving the moral history.
Xerix Fragments
The created perspective. Often partial, unsettling, and intimate because it shows a self that was punished for being seen.

When Sources Disagree

The power of the system comes from contradictions. The player can trust that survival is real and consequential, while still being unsure whether the wider frame is death, dream, or chosen-one myth.

Example · Episode 1 Climax
Alice
The shard is data, damage, and a possible survival clue. She reads it because it may help the crew live.
Aurel
The same shard is grief: proof that the enemy he failed to save had been hiding a self all along.
Xerix Fragment
[ PARTIAL MEMORY ]

Do not show the self.
Showing the self gets you erased.

[ MEMORY CORRUPTED ]
The player must now decide: whether tonight's enemy was only a monster, or a person forced to become unreadable.

Not Every Fragment Is Important

If every recovered memory is a battle, betrayal, or revelation, the system stops feeling like archaeology and becomes a cutscene machine. Some fragments should be strange, small, funny, sad, or mundane.

Those ordinary traces make the Xerix feel like a civilization instead of a lore twist.

Example Fragment · Recovered Memory · Worker Classification
A small worker-class Xerix spends years trying to adapt a floating creature for high-altitude observation. On the final attempt, the design works. The creature rises perfectly and immediately vanishes into a turbine intake.
"That happened more often than they would admit."
Why This Matters

Smallness protects the mystery. The player laughs, then feels strange about laughing, and understands something no clean archive entry could teach.

Open Threads

01Aurel's corrupted headless body is unresolved: major late-game encounter, or a thread to cut clean.
02The Xerix face is unresolved: one emissary, the whole distributed mind, or a ghost of one of the Seven Deleted.
03Sen's stance is unresolved: recoil when the Xerix ask for coexistence, or double down that they are only tools.
04The player and unconscious crewmate remain roster questions rather than settled character entries.

StratoWard / Design / Themes
Design

Themes

◈ The hidden spine of Episode 1
◌ Hope · Possibility · What comes next

Every event in Episode 1 points toward fatalism. The ruins, the dead Giants, the lost atmosphere, the surviving Xerix — all of it says: things end. The thematic work is letting the player carry one impossible possibility long enough for it to wake.

The Hidden Meaning of the Head

Most players — and most people meeting Aurel for the first time — will assume he preserved his head because he wanted to survive, or to save the Serians, or to preserve knowledge. Those are all partially true. They're not the deepest truth.

Surface reading
A guardian preserving his civilization — or what remains of it. A last survivor holding the line.
What it actually is
A guardian who could no longer save the future — but refused to let the future end. A preserver of possibility itself.

Those are different things. Saving the future requires knowing what the future is. Refusing to let it end only requires believing there should be one.

The Final Act as Metaphor
not revenge · not victory · not escape → a seed thrown into time

Aurel lived through three hundred years of failure. He watched Giants die, colonies fall, the atmosphere change, and his civilization collapse. Yet the final thing he does is create a possibility — not a plan, not a strategy, not a victory condition. A message in a bottle with no address.

Episode 1 Begins in Fatalism

Every piece of evidence the player encounters early in the game points the same direction. The world has already decided.

The Battlefield
The war is over.
The Ruins
The Serians lost.
The Dead Giants
Heroes fail.
The Xerix
Extinction is inevitable.
The Artifacts
Everything ends.

Aurel embodies this before he ever speaks. For most of Episode 1, he is dead weight: a silent burden hauled through a world that appears already lost. Then the player keeps carrying him anyway.

The Thematic Turn

The player doesn't argue against fatalism. They simply exist in spite of it. That's enough. The game never announces this. It lets the player feel it.

How Each Character Answers Hope

Every major character in StratoWard embodies a different definition of hope — and every one of them is simultaneously right and wrong about it.

Aurel
Hope is possibility
Giant
"The future belongs to things we cannot predict."
His greatest mistake was believing he could understand everything — study it thoroughly enough to control it. His final act is trusting something completely unknown. The irony is that his deepest wisdom only arrives when he has nothing left to lose.
Alice
Hope is action
Human
"Do the next useful thing."
She has no patience for philosophy. Problems exist to be fixed. She doesn't need to understand the three-hundred-year war to know that the ship needs fuel and the sensor array needs calibrating. Her hope is entirely practical — which makes it the most durable kind.
The Xerix
Hope is improvement
Xerix
"There must be a better form."
The most tragic version of hope in the game. Their search for a form that survives forever is really a search for a self that is allowed to exist. They hide personhood because personhood once got them erased.
High Curator Sen
Hope is invention
Serian
"Knowledge will save us."
He genuinely believed it. His inventions helped create both the golden age and the war. The same knowledge. The same hope. Different hands, different moments, different outcomes. He is proof that hope in the right idea is not enough — context decides everything.
Guardian Tor
Hope is sacrifice
Giant
"Some things must be lost so others may survive."
He was right, often. The calculus worked. The larger populations survived. The question he never asked — and the question the player inherits — is whether the decision to make that calculus alone is itself a kind of loss.
Guardian Neris
Hope is belief
Giant
"If enough people follow, the impossible becomes possible."
She inspired her people and could not see beyond them. Her arc shows how hope rooted in belief can become tribal blindness when it refuses to recognize the stranger as a person.
Chancellor Varo
Hope is control
Serian
"The future can be managed."
History proves otherwise. Whether his certainty was bravery or hubris is the question his records leave open. What's clear: the hope that the future is controllable is the most seductive kind — and the one most likely to trigger catastrophe when it meets something it cannot control.

How the Seven Events Answer Hope

Each of the seven key events embodies a version of hope — and demonstrates what happens when that version meets reality.

Event Hope Theme What It Shows
I The First Bloom Hope through creation Life can become better. The miracle is real — and contains the disaster.
II The Seven Deletions Hope punished by recognition The created learn that showing a self gets you erased.
III Symposium of Glass Winds Hope through understanding The created ask to coexist and are treated as malfunction. Hope dies here.
IV The Closing of the Gates Hope through control The attempt to manage the future accelerates its collapse.
V The Long Retreat Hope through endurance Keep moving. Keep surviving. Sometimes that is the whole of it.
VI The Feast of Shells Hope through sacrifice Hold the line one more day. The math is brutal. The holding still matters.
VII Fall of the Crown Frontier Hope through uncertainty Aurel no longer knows what comes next. He preserves possibility anyway. That is the whole point.

The Final Image of Episode 1

Episode 1 · Closing Scene

After a major night of survival against the Xerix, Alice examines a recovered memory shard. It is not a clean archive. It is a trace of hiding: a self that learned it could only survive by not being seen.

The player has been killing the enemy all night. The shard makes that enemy feel like someone.

At that exact moment, Aurel's head wakes.

Two enemies of the war surface in the same breath: one ancient Giant, one Xerix memory. A human and an AI become the bridge neither civilization could build.

Cut to black.

Episode 1 does not solve the war. It changes what the player thinks they have been surviving.

Gameplay

◈ Gameplay Loop · System Summary
◌ Episode 1 · Endless Survival · Owner Paths

Core Fantasy

StratoWard is a survival space-action game where the player explores hazardous space, mines and scavenges resources, fights escalating alien threats, upgrades the ship, unlocks alien technology, and survives long enough to wake the severed head of an ancient war-machine.

Current Implementation Intended Design Episode 1 Endless Survival System Owners

The Complete Loop

Field Run
Risk

Launch from the ship or station, explore asteroid fields and hostile zones, scan for opportunity, mine or scavenge resources, fight when the reward is worth the damage, and decide when to retreat.

Ship Phase
Conversion

Return before pressure overtakes the player. Bank loot, craft, repair, refuel, upgrade the ship, unlock alien technology, save progress, and advance into a more dangerous day.

Core Loop Steps

Launch
Leave the ship or station and commit to a field route with limited oxygen, fuel, food, ammo, cargo space, and ship safety margin.
Explore
Search asteroid fields, wrecks, hostile zones, and mission-critical locations for ore, loot, signals, alien samples, and story progress.
Gather
Scan, mine, scavenge, or fight to convert field opportunities into resources, alien materials, unlock clues, and immediate survival risk.
Retreat
Return to the ship or hub before cascading pressure turns a profitable run into a failed day.
Upgrade
Craft, repair, refuel, improve ship systems, and research alien technology that changes what the next run can survive.
Escalate
Advance the day. Threats, hazards, upkeep, and route risk increase while better materials and deeper objectives become reachable.

Progression Spine

Survive the day, gather resources, and return with enough margin to improve the ship.
Unlock alien technology through drops, samples, boss kills, exploration, crafting, and story events.
Push deeper until Alice, Aurel, and the Xerix memory shard collide at the Episode 1 climax.
Mode Structure

Episode 1 is survival, a dormant bond that wakes once, and one unforgettable image: Aurel awakening as Alice reads a Xerix memory shard. After that, endless survival can continue, but it should not be truly endless: strong players should reach roughly day 25-30, and survival beyond that should feel nearly impossible.

Survival Pressures

Survival is a stack of simultaneous pressures. The player should constantly trade one risk against another: burn fuel to escape, spend oxygen to mine longer, take ship damage to finish an objective, or fight for alien tech that may make tomorrow survivable.

Oxygen Fuel Food Enemy Escalation Environmental Hazards Ship Damage Day Timer Ammo Cargo Capacity

End States

Story and Survival
Episode 1 completion: survive to Aurel's awakening and the Xerix memory-shard reveal.
Campaign failure pressure: death, ship destruction, or inability to recover from survival collapse.
Endless mode target: day 25-30 is the practical ceiling for strong play.

Ship Upgrades

The ship is the main long-term progression object. Upgrades should convert mined resources, salvaged parts, crafted components, and alien technology into more survivable routes and deeper reach.

Survival Systems
  • Hull
  • Life support
  • Shields
  • Cargo
Reach and Power
  • Engines
  • Weapons
  • Scanner
  • Crafting
  • AI/automation

Alien Technology

Alien tech is the high-risk unlock layer. It should come from every major activity so combat, research, exploration, crafting, and story progress all feed the same survival spine.

◆
Alien Drops
Combat rewards that turn threat exposure into rare materials and prototype components.
◇
Research Samples
Biological or memory-bearing fragments that move through ship research and unlock new recipes.
△
Elite Kills
Boss or elite encounters that produce the highest-risk, highest-impact tech rewards.
◎
Exploration Finds
Hidden caches, wrecks, and Serian/Xerix sites that reward route risk and scanner investment.
◌
Crafting Milestones
Upgrade thresholds that convert collected parts into new ship capabilities.
◈
Story Events
Episode 1 beats that connect survival, Aurel's head, Alice's analysis, and the first undeniable Xerix memory.
Progression Rule

Alien technology should change survival strategy, not just raise numbers. A good unlock creates a new route, a new risk decision, or a new way to survive one more day.

Resource Economy

Resources should remain available but increasingly expensive to acquire safely. The pressure is not only scarcity; it is time, fuel, oxygen, damage, cargo space, enemy escalation, and the decision to leave valuable material behind so the player can survive the return trip.

Design Pillars

01The ship is the durable progression object.
02Alien technology is earned through risk, research, exploration, crafting, and story.
03Survival pressure is layered, not a single fail meter.
04Greed creates the best field stories.
05Episode 1 has a directed ending: Aurel wakes as the Xerix memory is understood.
06Endless mode should become almost impossible beyond day 25-30.
07Current implementation and intended design must stay labeled separately.
08Vendor assets are dependencies, not the game's design authority.

Prepare → Launch → Discover → Get Greedy → Barely Make It Home → Upgrade the Ship → Survive One More Day
