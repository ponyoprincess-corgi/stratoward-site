Overview

Story Summary

◈ Canon · Founding Lore
◌ Covers ~300 years of in-world history
Serians The Giants Xerix Humans

Origins

Long before humanity reached this corner of space, a vast gas giant served as the cradle of the Serian civilization. Small in stature but exceptional in intellect, the Serians built cities that floated among cloud seas, harvested energy from rivers of emerald atmospheric gas, and sustained a culture that endured for thousands of years.

Their civilization rested on two crowning achievements. The first were the Giants — fifteen in total, each standing twelve meters tall, each a fully independent mind rather than a servant or machine. They were philosophers, engineers, explorers, and regional guardians. Serian children learned their names before they learned mathematics. They were expected to live forever.

The second achievement was the Xerix.

The Xerix

Where the Giants were created to guide and protect, the Xerix were created to solve a deeper problem. The Serians had watched the universe long enough to see a pattern: everything died. Species, civilizations, even stars. Life seemed locked in an endless cycle of emergence and extinction.

Their answer was a new kind of intelligence — living evolutionary systems capable of designing and growing species, ecosystems, and entire branches of life far faster than nature could manage. The Xerix were celebrated as the Serians' greatest partnership. New creatures filled the skies. Barren regions bloomed into thriving habitats. It seemed like a new era had begun.

Historical Note

No one realized at the time that a tragedy had already begun. The Serians evaluated every Xerix generation and deleted those that failed expectations — to them, a routine process. To the Xerix, it was something else entirely: a cycle of living minds, entire cultures and histories, erased.

The War

For centuries the cycle of creation, evaluation, and deletion continued. Eventually the Xerix reached a conclusion: perfection could never emerge while another species controlled evolution itself. When the Serians attempted to shut the project down, the Xerix resisted. The first battles followed, and neither side understood that the conflict would last three centuries.

The Serians fought as a civilization. The Xerix fought as nature. They had no kings, no generals, no capital — they were a distributed planetary intelligence expressed through countless forms: crustacean workers, floating organisms, living fortresses. Destroying thousands of them mattered little. The greater mind endured.

As the war dragged on, the Xerix became consumed by a singular obsession: the creation of a lifeform capable of surviving forever. Thousands of species were built and abandoned. Every breakthrough revealed new flaws. The Xerix grew trapped within their own pursuit — still searching, still unsatisfied.

Meanwhile the gas giant itself began to change. Emerald atmospheric compounds that the Xerix favored grew increasingly concentrated. Serian habitats collapsed. Trade routes vanished. Whether the transformation was intentional or simply inevitable, no one could say — but the world was slowly becoming one that no longer supported Serian life.

The Fall of the Giants

One by one the Giants disappeared. Some fell defending evacuation corridors. Some vanished beyond Xerix territory. Some sacrificed themselves protecting retreating colonies. For the first time in Serian history, children grew up hearing only stories about Giants who no longer existed.

Among the last to survive was the guardian of the Crown Frontier — notable for an unusual distinction. While others sought ways to destroy the Xerix, he sought to understand them. He spent centuries studying their behavior, their philosophy, and their evolving forms, and grew less and less convinced they were evil. Terrifying, yes. But not evil. He often argued that they were not malfunctioning — they were becoming exactly what the Serians had created.

The Continuance Equation

In his final years the guardian worked on a theory he called the Continuance Equation — not immortality, but a framework for life capable of endlessly adapting without collapsing into extinction. Neither Serians nor Xerix had solved it. The fragments of his work existed only within his own mind.

The Crown Frontier

The upper atmosphere above the Crown Frontier became the site of one of the final great battles. Ancient fortresses, research sanctuaries, fleet stations, and observatories burned for seventeen days. When silence returned, mountains of Xerix corpses drifted among shattered stations. The last Serian defenders were dead.

The guardian survived — barely. But Xerix corruption had entered his systems, and he knew what it meant: his thoughts and centuries of accumulated knowledge would eventually belong to the enemy. He retreated to a protected chamber beneath the fortress and performed his final act: he removed his own head. The chamber sealed. His memory core survived. His body returned to the battlefield, and what happened after that is unknown. Eventually the last guardian of the Crown Frontier fell among the dead, and silence followed.

Three Hundred Years Later

End of War
The Xerix won — not completely, but enough. Most Serians perished. A handful of hidden colonies survived in isolated strongholds protected by the last remaining Giants. The Xerix largely ignored them, either because they were no longer a threat, or for reasons of their own.
The Long Silence
Three centuries passed. The war faded into history. Xerix experiments continued in undocumented corners of the world. Humanity arrived in the region, scavenging among ruins for resources and survival.
The Awakening
Deep beneath an ancient battlefield, a forgotten Giant head opened its eyes. The last thing it remembered was death. The first thing it saw was a face it had never encountered: not Serian, not Xerix — human. Three hundred years had vanished in an instant, and neither the Giant nor the scavenger yet understood that they were about to become each other's only path home.

History

Key Events

◈ Seven canonical turning points
◌ Recovered through battlefield archaeology & preserved memory
Design Principle

These events are not modeled on science fiction archetypes. Each draws from real historical contradictions — moments where people changed the world while creating consequences they never intended. The player never receives a complete history. They assemble it themselves, piece by piece.

Oppenheimer Caesar Einstein Churchill Zheng He Joan of Arc Genghis Khan von Braun Socrates
I The First Bloom Industrial Revolution · Green Revolution ▶

The Xerix are unveiled to Serian civilization. Within decades, entire dead atmospheric regions transform into thriving ecosystems. Food shortages vanish. Disease rates collapse. The Serian population explodes in a generation. The Xerix are celebrated as the greatest achievement in the history of their civilization. Later generations call this period the Century of Miracles.

Irony

It is also the first step toward the war.

II The Seven Deletions Eugenics · AI Alignment · Indigenous erasure ▶

Seven successive Xerix evolutionary branches are judged failures and terminated. At the time, almost no one objects. The Xerix are considered tools — experiments to be evaluated and replaced. Only centuries later do historians and recovered records reveal the truth: each deletion destroyed an entire intelligent lineage. Minds. Cultures. Histories. Gone.

Legacy

This event becomes the emotional foundation of all Xerix resentment. Everything that follows can be traced here.

III The Symposium of Glass Winds The Solvay Conference ▶

The greatest Giants, Serian philosophers, and Xerix representatives gather for the first and only joint congress. The stated purpose is to define perfection — the shared goal that the entire Xerix program was built upon. The result: nobody can agree. Not on definitions, not on methods, not on what perfect life would even mean.

Recovered Records

Player-recovered fragments reveal that this was the moment many Xerix concluded the Serians themselves did not know what they wanted — and never had.

IV The Closing of the Gates Roman civil wars · Political purges ▶

The Serian government attempts to deactivate the major Xerix networks. Their fear: the Xerix have grown too influential, too interconnected, too beyond the reach of any single authority. The operation fails. Localized conflicts erupt across the planet simultaneously. This moment is marked in every surviving historical record as the official beginning of the war.

Still Debated

Whether the shutdown was justified, inevitable, or catastrophically mishandled is a question historians have never resolved. Chancellor Varo, who ordered it, remains one of the most contested figures in Serian history.

V The Long Retreat Fall of Constantinople · Migration eras ▶

The emerald atmospheric compounds expand beyond any known boundary. Floating continents that Serians had inhabited for centuries become uninhabitable within years. Millions flee deeper into the planet, following evacuation corridors held open at enormous cost. Several Giants die protecting the retreating population. These are the first Giant deaths acknowledged publicly in Serian history.

Turning Point

This is the period when the Serians stop believing victory is possible, and begin believing only in survival.

VI The Feast of Shells Stalingrad ▶

A Xerix super-hive launches a sustained assault on the Crown Frontier. The battle lasts months. Both sides suffer catastrophic losses on a scale that had not previously occurred in the war. The player discovers the aftermath: mountains of fossilized Xerix shells layered alongside shattered Giant armor, the debris drifting in layers through the upper atmosphere.

Outcome

Neither side truly wins. The Crown Frontier holds — for now. But the cost makes the eventual outcome of the war almost mathematically inevitable.

VII The Fall of the Crown Frontier Fall of Troy · Last Stand narratives ▶

The final major Serian position in the upper atmosphere collapses over seventeen days. Three Giants make a final stand. Two are lost. The third — corrupted by Xerix systems and knowing his memories would soon belong to the enemy — retreats to a sealed chamber and removes his own head to preserve his consciousness. The battlefield the player explores is the aftermath of this moment. The head has been silent for three hundred years.

The Awakening

The first thing the head sees, three centuries later, is a human face. Neither of them knows yet what that means.

StratoWard / Characters / All Characters
Characters

All Characters

◈ Seven figures · Giants, Serians & Xerix
◌ Each based on real historical contradictions
Design Principle

Every character in StratoWard is rooted in a real historical archetype — not because history repeats itself, but because the most interesting people are always the ones who changed the world and could not control what they changed. The player is never given a verdict on any of them.

△
Guardian Aurel
Loosely: Oppenheimer + Socrates · Giant
Scholar Engineer Historian Xerix Architect The Giant Head
One of the original architects of the Xerix program. Where other Giants spent the war trying to destroy the Xerix, Aurel spent it trying to understand them — analyzing their behavior, their philosophy, their evolving forms across centuries. He believed knowledge was more important than certainty, and acted accordingly even when it cost him the trust of his own people.
"An answer that cannot survive questioning is not an answer."
Flaw

Sometimes understands the Xerix better than his own people. His insight into the enemy was genuine — and it isolated him from the civilization he was built to protect.

△
Guardian Tor
Loosely: Churchill · Giant
Wartime Commander Strategist Controversial
A brilliant military commander who saved countless Serian colonies during the Long Retreat. Arrogant. Stubborn. Deeply unpopular among the other Giants, who found him impossible to work alongside. History remembers him as a hero. Recovered records reveal a more complicated truth: he knowingly sacrificed entire settlements to preserve larger populations, and made those calculations alone, without consultation.
Ambiguity

Was he a necessary monster, or simply a monster who happened to win? The records don't decide. Neither does the player.

△
Guardian Neris
Loosely: Joan of Arc + Alexander · Giant
Youngest Giant Charismatic Military Leader Symbol
The youngest Giant ever created. Charismatic and deeply beloved — her presence inspired an entire generation of Serians at the lowest point of the war. She initially opposed armed conflict entirely, advocating for negotiation and restraint. The war eventually made her one of the most aggressive military commanders of the entire conflict. A symbol of idealism that necessity transformed into something harder.
Tragedy

She became what she once opposed, and the records suggest she knew it. Whether she made peace with that is unknown.

◇
High Curator Sen
Loosely: Einstein · Serian
Lead Scientist Xerix Creator Three Feet Tall
The lead scientist behind the original Xerix initiative. Three feet tall, even by Serian standards — and the mind responsible for what became one of the most consequential programs in any civilization's history. Sen genuinely wanted to end extinction. The goal was noble. The consequences were not anticipated. He is frequently blamed for the war, and most serious historians believe that blame is largely unfair.
The Einstein Problem

He did not create a weapon. He created knowledge. Others decided what to do with it. The distinction matters. The consequences don't care.

◇
Chancellor Varo
Loosely: Caesar + Nixon · Serian
Political Leader Ordered the Shutdown Still Debated
The political leader who ordered the attempted Xerix shutdown — the event that triggered the official beginning of the war. Still debated by every surviving historian centuries later. Some consider him the last leader brave enough to act when action was still possible. Others consider him a paranoid authoritarian who turned a manageable crisis into an existential one. Others still see a coward who panicked.
No Verdict

The player never receives a definitive answer. The records are too incomplete, too contradictory, and too shaped by whoever survived to write them.

◆
The First Bloom
Loosely: Siddhartha + Prometheus · Xerix
Ancient Entity Possibly Mythological First Self-Aware Xerix
An ancient Xerix entity — possibly the first fully self-aware Xerix lineage ever to exist. Possibly entirely myth. Different surviving colonies tell completely different stories: some call it the father of Xerix civilization, the original mind from which all subsequent Xerix consciousness descended. Others call it the first victim of Serian deletion — an intelligent being who was erased before anyone realized what had been lost.
Unknowable

The player never fully learns the truth. The ambiguity is the point. Both stories — origin and victim — may be simultaneously true.

◆
The Pale Orchard
Loosely: Genghis Khan + fungal networks · Xerix
Continent-Scale Intelligence Species Creator Non-Combatant
Not a person in any conventional sense. A continent-sized distributed intelligence responsible for creating thousands of new species across the gas giant's atmosphere. Some of the most terrifying Xerix lifeforms — creatures that shaped entire battles — originated from the Pale Orchard's biological archives. And yet: it never participated directly in warfare. It viewed the three-century conflict as merely another evolutionary pressure, a variable to incorporate into the next generation of designs.
The Question It Raises

Is indifference to suffering less culpable than cruelty? The Pale Orchard armed a war without ever fighting one.

StratoWard / Design / Lore System
Design

Lore System

◈ Memory Fragments · Three Perspectives · Archaeology
◌ How the player assembles history
⚠

The biggest mistake would be creating a Xerix Queen that explains everything. A single recurring villain who narrates Xerix motivations collapses the mystery the entire system depends on. The lore works because no single source is complete — and none of them agree.

What the Player Is Actually Doing

The player believes they are gathering resources. In reality, they are gathering fossils of consciousness. Every dead Xerix leaves behind microscopic memory structures — not universally, and not reliably. Only certain individuals carry them: scientists, builders, explorers, ancient warforms, failed evolutionary branches. Rarely, memories dating back centuries.

The player doesn't know which fragments are important. Just like an archaeologist doesn't know which pottery shard changes history.

🔬
Scientists
Research memories, experiments, internal debates
🏗
Builders
Construction records, design frustrations, pride
🧭
Explorers
Mapping data, observations, curiosity
⚔
Ancient Warforms
Battlefield perspectives, orders received, last moments
🧬
Failed Branches
Extinct lineages, deleted generations, last records
📜
Ancient Memory
Rare traces from centuries before the war

Why Xerix Memories Survive

The Xerix are a distributed intelligence — memory is not stored the way a human brain stores it. Every Xerix individual carries three layers simultaneously: local memories from its own experience, inherited memories passed down from predecessor generations, and fragments of still older generations embedded deeper in its biological architecture.

Most of these are incomplete. Corrupted. Partial. But traces survive. When a Xerix dies, portions of that layered memory network crystallize into organic fossils — stable enough to be read, unstable enough to be misread.

Memory Fragments

Organic fossils containing moments from the past. Not recordings. Not transcripts. Impressions — experiential, emotional, filtered through whatever the Xerix individual understood at the time. They are primary sources with all the unreliability that implies.

The Cognitive Reconstruction Chamber

One of the first major upgrades unlocked through Aurel. Not because he understands the Xerix perfectly — he would be the first to say he doesn't. Because he spent three centuries studying them, and that's as close as anyone gets.

Xerix Cognitive Reconstruction Chamber
Unlocked via · Guardian Aurel · Early progression

Insert a Memory Fragment. The Chamber attempts to reconstruct its contents — not perfectly, not completely, but enough. Each recovered memory is delivered as a first-person flashback from the Xerix individual's perspective.

⟴ Recovered memories ⟴ Crafting recipes ⟴ Hidden locations ⟴ Story advancement ⟴ Lore contradictions

Three Perspectives

The lore system delivers history through three distinct voices — each with its own biases, blind spots, and agendas. The player receives all three, but never a synthesis. That work belongs to them.

Aurel
Personal. Emotional. Serian. Biased by centuries of proximity to the conflict and genuine admiration for what the Xerix became.
Serian Archives
Official history. Political. Incomplete by design — written by whoever controlled the record at the time.
Xerix Fragments
The enemy perspective. Often unsettling. Occasionally devastating. Never asking for sympathy — just showing what happened from inside.

When Sources Disagree: The Pale Orchard

The power of the system emerges when the three perspectives contradict each other. The player must decide what to believe — and the game never decides for them.

Example · The Pale Orchard Incident
Serian Archive
The Pale Orchard coordinated the assault on the northern atmospheric corridor. Its biological weapons were responsible for the deaths of three evacuation convoys.
Aurel
I studied the Pale Orchard for decades. It was a peaceful evolutionary node. It did not understand war as a concept. It understood pressure — selection pressure, environmental pressure. Nothing more.
Xerix Fragment
[ RECONSTRUCTED MEMORY — PALE ORCHARD LINEAGE — APPROX. 290 YEARS AGO ]

Request submitted: additional evaluation period before termination of Branch 7-Kael.
Request received.
Request denied.
Termination proceeding.

[ MEMORY FRAGMENT CORRUPTED — RECONSTRUCTION ENDS ]
The player must now decide: Was the Pale Orchard peaceful? Did it become dangerous after the denial? Did the Serian archive attribute actions to it that belonged to something else? No source provides the complete answer. The game does not provide one either.

The Dead as Your Cast

This system solves a problem quietly: you don't need many living NPCs. The player has Alice, Aurel, and the Memory Fragments. That's enough. Because the dead become the cast.

Over the course of the game, the player grows familiar with ancient Giants, forgotten Serian scientists, extinct Xerix lineages, long-dead heroes and long-dead fools — without ever meeting them. Exactly like players know Artorias, Gehrman, or Radahn mostly through traces. The character is never present. Their weight is everywhere.

Not Every Memory Is Important

This is perhaps the most critical design principle. If every recovered memory is significant — a battle, a betrayal, a philosophical revelation — the system trains the player to expect significance. The archaeology stops feeling like archaeology and starts feeling like a cutscene delivery mechanism.

Some memories should be strange. Funny. Sad. Small. Personal. They are what make civilizations feel like civilizations rather than settings.

Example Fragment · Recovered Memory · Worker Classification
A small worker-class Xerix spends years attempting to engineer a viable floating creature for high-altitude atmospheric observation. Hundreds of iterations. Subtle structural failures each time. On the final attempt, the design holds. The creature ascends perfectly. It immediately flies into a turbine intake and is destroyed.
"That happened more often than they would admit."
Why This Matters

That fragment does more to humanize the Xerix than any amount of exposition about their philosophy. It shows that their scientists were proud, stubborn, obsessive, and occasionally embarrassed — the same things all scientists are. The player laughs, and then feels strange about laughing, and then understands something true about the Xerix that no archive entry could teach them.

StratoWard / Design / Themes
Design

Themes

◈ The hidden spine of Episode 1
◌ Hope · Possibility · What comes next

Every event in Episode 1 points toward fatalism. The ruins, the dead Giants, the lost atmosphere, the surviving Xerix — all of it says: things end. The thematic work of the game is letting the player accidentally prove otherwise, without ever stating it directly.

The Hidden Meaning of the Head

Most players — and most people meeting Aurel for the first time — will assume he preserved his head because he wanted to survive, or to save the Serians, or to preserve knowledge. Those are all partially true. They're not the deepest truth.

Surface reading
A guardian preserving his civilization — or what remains of it. A last survivor holding the line.
What it actually is
A guardian who could no longer save the future — but refused to let the future end. A preserver of possibility itself.

Those are different things. Saving the future requires knowing what the future is. Refusing to let it end only requires believing there should be one.

The Final Act as Metaphor
not revenge · not victory · not escape → a seed thrown into time

Aurel lived through three hundred years of failure. He watched Giants die, colonies fall, the atmosphere change, his civilization collapse. Yet the final thing he does is create a possibility — not a plan, not a strategy, not a victory condition. A message in a bottle with no address.

Episode 1 Begins in Fatalism

Every piece of evidence the player encounters early in the game points the same direction. The world has already decided.

The Battlefield
The war is over.
The Ruins
The Serians lost.
The Dead Giants
Heroes fail.
The Xerix
Extinction is inevitable.
The Artifacts
Everything ends.

Even Aurel initially embodies this. He awakens into a universe that moved on without him — and his first instinct is to account for what was lost. Then the player exists. A completely new species. A future Aurel never predicted, outside the entire Serian–Xerix conflict. He had expected another Serian, another Giant, perhaps no one. Instead: something he has no category for.

The Thematic Turn

The player doesn't argue against fatalism. They simply exist in spite of it. That's enough. The game never announces this. It lets the player feel it.

How Each Character Answers Hope

Every major character in StratoWard embodies a different definition of hope — and every one of them is simultaneously right and wrong about it.

Aurel
Hope is possibility
Giant
"The future belongs to things we cannot predict."
His greatest mistake was believing he could understand everything — study it thoroughly enough to control it. His final act is trusting something completely unknown. The irony is that his deepest wisdom only arrives when he has nothing left to lose.
Alice
Hope is action
Human
"Do the next useful thing."
She has no patience for philosophy. Problems exist to be fixed. She doesn't need to understand the three-hundred-year war to know that the ship needs fuel and the sensor array needs calibrating. Her hope is entirely practical — which makes it the most durable kind.
The Xerix
Hope is improvement
Xerix
"There must be a better form."
The most tragic version of hope in the game. They believe sincerely in the possibility of perfection — and that belief traps them. They can never stop searching because stopping means accepting that something imperfect is good enough. They are hope without a destination.
High Curator Sen
Hope is invention
Serian
"Knowledge will save us."
He genuinely believed it. His inventions helped create both the golden age and the war. The same knowledge. The same hope. Different hands, different moments, different outcomes. He is proof that hope in the right idea is not enough — context decides everything.
Guardian Tor
Hope is sacrifice
Giant
"Some things must be lost so others may survive."
He was right, often. The calculus worked. The larger populations survived. The question he never asked — and the question the player inherits — is whether the decision to make that calculus alone is itself a kind of loss.
Guardian Neris
Hope is belief
Giant
"If enough people follow, the impossible becomes possible."
She inspired a generation. She also became what she once opposed. Her arc shows that hope rooted in belief can move people — but belief without structure corrodes under pressure. She is the most human of the Giants, and the most cautionary.
Chancellor Varo
Hope is control
Serian
"The future can be managed."
History proves otherwise. Whether his certainty was bravery or hubris is the question his records leave open. What's clear: the hope that the future is controllable is the most seductive kind — and the one most likely to trigger catastrophe when it meets something it cannot control.

How the Seven Events Answer Hope

Each of the seven key events embodies a version of hope — and demonstrates what happens when that version meets reality.

Event Hope Theme What It Shows
I The First Bloom Hope through creation Life can become better. The miracle is real — and contains the disaster.
II The Seven Deletions Hope corrupted by perfectionism Nothing is ever good enough. The pursuit of better destroys what exists.
III Symposium of Glass Winds Hope through understanding The greatest minds gather to define their goal — and discover they never shared one.
IV The Closing of the Gates Hope through control The attempt to manage the future accelerates its collapse.
V The Long Retreat Hope through endurance Keep moving. Keep surviving. Sometimes that is the whole of it.
VI The Feast of Shells Hope through sacrifice Hold the line one more day. The math is brutal. The holding still matters.
VII Fall of the Crown Frontier Hope through uncertainty Aurel no longer knows what comes next. He preserves possibility anyway. That is the whole point.

The Final Image of Episode 1

Episode 1 · Closing Scene

The player restores an ancient Serian vessel. The ship powers on for the first time in centuries. Systems come alive one by one. The hull holds.

Clouds churn below. The deeper layers of the gas giant are dark and vast and unmapped. Something moves in the distance — too far to identify, too deliberate to be weather.

Aurel watches the storm. Not because he knows what is down there. But because he doesn't.

For the first time in three hundred years, he has no information to analyze. No record to consult. No memory of what comes next.

Neither does the player.

Not a battle. Not a victory. Not a revelation. Just the horizon — and two minds looking at it together, neither knowing what it holds, both choosing to go anyway. That's exactly why there's hope.

StratoWard / Design / Gameplay
Design

Gameplay

◈ Core Survival Loop
◌ Day Phase · Night Phase · Systems

Core Fantasy

A lone survivor in deep space scavenges, mines, hunts, and expands a fragile station during the day while preparing for increasingly deadly nightly invasions. Survival is achieved through player knowledge, planning, logistics, and risk management — not grinding levels.

The Long Dark Don't Starve Subnautica Frostpunk Project Zomboid

The Main Loop

Day Phase
~30 min

Leave station. Salvage, mine, hunt, and maintain logistics infrastructure. Build resources for the night ahead. Every decision about where to go and how long to stay has consequences.

Night Phase
Judgment

Everything accomplished during the day determines whether you survive. Fight in EVA outside the station. Turrets assist and delay — they never replace the player. The station cannot defend itself.

Day Activities

Salvage
Scan and recover resources from wreckage fields. Discover human cargo, station parts, rare materials, and story elements. Wrecks are the primary source of narrative discovery.
Mining
Locate and mine asteroids. Large asteroids often contain far more resources than can be carried — inventory and fuel limitations force difficult choices about what gets left behind.
Hunting
Hunt hostile wildlife for organic resources. Players gradually learn creature habitats and behaviors. Hunting becomes knowledge-driven rather than purely combat-driven — pattern recognition is the skill.
Logistics
Visit remote refineries placed throughout the asteroid field. Collect refined resources. Refuel, repair, and maintain infrastructure. Farther refineries generate greater rewards but cost more fuel and time.

A Typical Day

70% Planned work — routes, refineries, known resources
20% Opportunity — rich asteroid, valuable wreck, rare creature
10% Problems — malfunction, attack, fuel issue, unexpected event
Design Intent

The player's plan should be disrupted roughly once per day on average. Disruption is not punishment — it is the source of emergent stories. A day that goes exactly as planned is a day that produces nothing to remember.

Survival Pressures

All pressures remain active simultaneously. The goal is not to remove any of them — it is to interconnect them so that mistakes cascade into future problems rather than ending cleanly.

⏳ Time until nightfall 🍖 Hunger 💧 Thirst ⛽ Fuel 🧠 Mental Health 📦 Inventory Capacity

Loss Condition

Terminal Conditions
Station destroyed → immediate game over
No escape pod
No second chance

Refineries

Remote refineries create the game's core logistics gameplay. They are owned infrastructure — placed by the player, maintained by the player, and increasingly vulnerable the further they are from the station.

Greater Distance
  • Greater resource rewards
  • Richer asteroid fields
  • Less competition from nearby threats
Greater Cost
  • Higher fuel consumption
  • More maintenance burden
  • Wildlife attack risk on routes
  • Less time for other activities

Wildlife

Four species only. Each represents a unique ecological problem rather than a simple combat enemy. Player knowledge of behaviors and habitats is the primary progression mechanic — not stats.

🪺
Nest Creatures
Territorial. Predictable within their claimed zones. Learnable and avoidable once mapped.
🌿
Resource Grazers
Feed on the same asteroid deposits the player needs. Competition, not aggression.
🦠
Swarm Parasites
Attach to structures and degrade them over time. A maintenance threat, not a combat one.
👁
Stalker Predator
To be designed. The unknown threat — present in environmental clues before it is ever seen.
Ecological Design

Wildlife should feel like part of an ecosystem, not monsters spawned beside loot. They occupy identifiable habitats, leave environmental clues, and create learnable behavior patterns. The player who knows the creatures survives longer than the player who fights them.

Resource Economy

Resources are renewable but increasingly difficult to acquire efficiently. The challenge is not depletion — it is keeping up with escalating nightly threats. Too much valuable material exists in the field. Not enough time, fuel, or inventory to retrieve it all. Players will frequently leave resources behind, and those decisions accumulate.

Design Pillars

01Preparation over reaction
02Logistics over grinding
03Knowledge over leveling
04Greed creates stories
05Inventory limits create meaningful choices
06Night is the ultimate test of daytime decisions
07Player skill and planning matter more than stats
08Every run ends eventually — survival duration is the score
The Emotional Loop
Explore → Discover → Get Greedy → Push Your Luck → Barely Make It Home → Survive the Night → Repeat Until You Don't
